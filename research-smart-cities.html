Inclusivity in the Era of Smart Cities: Building Connected Communities for Everyone

Research Post  ·  University of North Carolina at Greensboro
Smart CitiesAccessibilityQuadruple Helix ModelDisabilitiesEJIS

The vision of a smart city promises a future of connected communities, sustainable infrastructures, and improved quality of life for its citizens. Yet a glaring oversight looms large in this futuristic landscape — the inclusion and accessibility of people with disabilities.

Understanding Smart Cities and the Role of Inclusivity

At their core, smart cities are not merely a collection of automated traffic lights or energy-efficient buildings. They are dynamic ecosystems powered by Internet of Things (IoT) devices, Information Communication Technology (ICT), and sophisticated data analytics. These interconnected systems collect and transmit data to cloud-based applications that analyze and manage urban information in real time.

However, while many of us effortlessly navigate this rapidly digitizing world via apps on our phones, a significant demographic is often left behind. People with disabilities, who could greatly benefit from these smart technologies, find themselves excluded due to inaccessible designs and implementations.

The Quadruple Helix Model: A Holistic Approach

To understand the multifaceted challenges and opportunities within this realm, this study employs the Quadruple Helix Model — a conceptual framework encompassing four primary sectors:

  1. Citizens — The heart of every city. Their needs, challenges, and interactions with urban tech shape the true essence of smart cities.
  2. Industry — The innovators who design, develop, and implement the smart technologies that power these cities.
  3. University — The knowledge hubs that drive research, development, and innovation in urban tech.
  4. Government — The regulators and facilitators, ensuring that technological advancements align with public welfare.

Core Research Questions

  • How do people with disabilities engage in their cities?
  • What issues do they encounter when engaging in city activities?
  • How do city governments engage citizens with disabilities in the development and procurement of smart technologies?
  • How could smart technologies be developed to better consider the needs of people with disabilities?
